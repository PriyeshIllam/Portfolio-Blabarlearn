01 · Project Brief

Design a website that makes players want to enter the world.

THRALL is a dark fantasy game built by a cross-disciplinary student team. Our FED24 group was tasked with designing and building the promotional website — a digital front door that had to communicate the game's atmosphere, introduce characters and storyline, and convert visitors into players.

Design Challenge: How do you translate a dark, immersive game world into a web experience that feels authentic — while remaining navigable, accessible, and conversion-focused?

This was a cross-disciplinary group project. The design process ran from a written content brief through two IA iterations, annotated mobile wireframes, and a dark-themed hi-fi prototype — all in Figma, with peer review integrated throughout. I also contributed to the front-end development of the shipped website.

My Contributions

  • Content brief and two information architecture iterations
  • Colour-coded user flow mapping (About / Play / Language)
  • Mobile wireframes — annotated with UX rationale at every decision point
  • Interaction states — hamburger menu (default/hover/pressed) and button states
  • Language switcher component (EN/Swedish, three interaction states)
  • Front-end development contribution to the shipped production website

Note on collaboration

This was a group project. The Figma screenshots show my individual design contributions. The live website was a team effort — individual contributor details are not shared here out of respect for team members' privacy.

03 · Information Architecture

Two iterations. One clearer structure.

The IA process moved from a content list to a navigable structure — then to a colour-coded flow that made the user journey legible to the whole cross-disciplinary team.

Iteration 1

Content Brief + Initial Sitemap

Started from a written content brief defining four areas: game header, about/trailer, gameplay GIFs, team. Two structural models explored — linear and hub-and-spoke — to find the right navigation logic for a game promotional site.

Iteration 2

Colour-coded User Flow

Refined into a three-section flow colour-coded by user goal: About (yellow), Play (pink), Language (green). Exposed a navigation gap in v1 — the Play section had no return path to landing — and fixed it. Added persistent scroll/home button.

Design rationale: Colour-coding by content section — not by page — made the structure immediately readable for game developers and artists who weren't UX-trained. It also made navigation gaps visible that prose descriptions would have hidden. This process discipline directly shaped the final site structure.

04 · Wireframes & Annotation

Mobile-first. Annotated at every decision point.

Wireframes were built mobile-first with sticky annotations documenting UX rationale and surfacing open questions for team review — preventing late-stage disagreements when development began.

Mobile Layout

Navigation + Character Cards

Hamburger menu with three interaction states explicitly designed. Character showcase as swipeable cards — reduces scroll on mobile, creates a game-like reveal that mirrors how players encounter characters in the game itself.

Play Page

Conversion Page + Button States

Play page built around a single CTA: "Play the Game." Three button states designed — default, hover (bold), press (dissolve effect). Desktop breakpoint shown alongside mobile to check cross-viewport consistency.

About + Language

Storyline, Concept + Language Toggle

About section structured as Storyline → Concept → Team. Language switcher designed with default/hover/pressed states for EN/Swedish. Character avatar row with consistent sizing for the team section.

05 · Key Design Decisions

What we decided and why.

Every annotation in the wireframes documents a real decision. These six shaped the design — and the live site — most.

🌑

Dark aesthetic as UX, not decoration

The dark background reduces eye strain during long scrolling and creates strong contrast for the gold/red type system. Every colour choice was validated against the game's visual language to ensure the site feels like a continuation of the game world.

Visual consistency
☰

Hamburger menu — three states, partial overlay

Default, hover, and pressed states designed explicitly. Menu overlay debated: full-screen vs partial. Chose partial coverage so the game atmosphere remains visible behind the menu, preserving immersion during navigation.

Interaction design
🃏

Swipeable character cards

Swipeable cards replace a scrollable list on mobile — reducing vertical scroll length and mirroring how players encounter characters in-game. The interaction pattern reinforces the game's mental model rather than breaking from it.

Mental model alignment
🌐

Bilingual structure from IA stage

EN/Swedish switching was designed into the IA — not added afterwards. Both language variants share identical structure, preventing the content gaps that typically appear when internationalisation is retrofitted onto an existing design.

Internationalisation
▶

Single CTA on the Play page

The Play page was deliberately stripped of competing actions. One button: "Play the Game." Peer reviewer feedback confirmed this. Reducing choice at the conversion point follows established decision architecture principles — and the annotation record shows it was conscious, not default.

Decision architecture
💬

Design and development in the same hands

Contributing to both Figma design and front-end code meant design decisions were validated against implementation constraints in real time. This reduced the gap between what was designed and what shipped — a practice I want to continue and theorise at master's level.

Design–dev continuity

06 · Hi-Fi Result

From wireframe to dark fantasy UI.

The final hi-fi prototype realised the IA and wireframe decisions in a full visual system consistent with the game's aesthetic identity.

Typography

Display font with a gothic character. Gold title treatment on dark backgrounds. Uppercase CTA text for drama and authority. All type choices validated against legibility on dark surfaces.

Colour System

Near-black backgrounds with gold and dark red accents. Mirrors the game's internal colour language — the website continues the game world rather than creating a separate promotional layer around it.

Navigation

Three primary nav items (THRALL / ABOUT / PLAY) directly reflect the IA structure. Language toggle persistent in the header. All navigation paths verified against the IA before implementation.

07 · Reflection

What this project taught me.

"Good IA is invisible to users — but without it, every design decision downstream becomes harder to justify."
  • The two-iteration IA process was the most valuable activity. Colour-coding by user goal rather than page made the structure immediately legible to team members who weren't designers.
  • Designing interaction states in wireframes — not leaving them to hi-fi — prevented behaviour disagreements late in the process and made the developer handoff much smoother.
  • Contributing to both design and development gave me direct knowledge of where design decisions are easy to implement and where they create friction. That awareness has changed how I approach early design choices.
  • Compared to PromptCraft, this project showed me that aesthetic coherence and structural soundness are not separate concerns — the dark UI only works because the IA underneath it is clear.

Peer Review in Practice

The Figma annotations preserve real design dialogue — reviewers questioning choices, confirming decisions, suggesting improvements. This is evidence-based collaborative design practice, not solo aesthetic judgement.

Reviewer question

"Could it maybe be another colour than just white? It's such a contrast from the overall page."

Design response

Menu overlay reconsidered → partial dark overlay chosen to preserve game atmosphere visibility.

Connection to Malmö — Interaction Design This project sits at the intersection of two things the programme emphasises: studio-based collaborative practice, and the relationship between designed artefacts and the experiences they produce. I want to bring this design-to-development fluency into research contexts at master's level.
